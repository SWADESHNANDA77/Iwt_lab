Sushruta: The Father of Surgery Sushruta, an ancient Indian physician and surgeon who flourished around 600 BCE in the city of Kashi (modern-day Varanasi), is widely revered as the "Father of Surgery" and the "Father of Plastic Surgery". His monumental treatise, the Sushruta Samhita (Sushruta's Compendium), is one of the most important foundational texts of Ayurveda and arguably the world's oldest text detailing complex surgical procedures. His work laid the groundwork for surgical practices that continue to influence modern medicine today. The Sushruta Samhita is a comprehensive text, documenting over 1,100 diseases, the use of hundreds of medicinal plants, and detailed instructions for more than 300 surgical procedures and 120 surgical instruments. Sushruta's approach was revolutionary for his time. He emphasized practical experience, advocating for the dissection of cadavers to understand anatomy, a practice that bypassed prevailing religious taboos. Students, known as Saushrutas, were required to take an oath similar to the modern Hippocratic Oath and practice incisions on vegetables and animal carcasses before operating on humans. Sushruta's pioneering techniques in plastic and reconstructive surgery are perhaps his most famous contribution. The Sushruta Samhita provides the first written record of a cheek-flap rhinoplasty, a technique used to reconstruct noses, which were often amputated as a form of punishment in ancient society. He described intricate methods for skin grafting, including using skin from the cheek or forehead, and techniques for otoplasty (ear reconstruction). European surgeons adapted these techniques in the 18th century, integrating them into modern plastic surgery. Beyond plastic surgery, Sushruta performed a wide range of operations, including cataract removals using a curved needle, cesareans, urinary stone removals, and setting complex bone fractures. His systematic approach extended to sanitation and hygiene, emphasizing cleanliness of instruments and the surgical environment long before the germ theory of disease was established in the West. He also suggested the use of herbal mixtures with wine for anesthesia to reduce pain during operations. Sushruta's influence transcended time and geography. His text was translated into Arabic in the 8th century CE as the Kitab-ul-Susrud and subsequently spread to the West, shaping medical traditions in Greece, Persia, and Europe. His principles of precision, hygiene, and holistic patient care laid an enduring foundation for surgical education and practice. Today, Sushruta's legacy is honored globally, with his methods and innovations continuing to resonate in the operating rooms of the 21st century. His work serves as a testament to the advanced state of medical knowledge in ancient India and solidifies his place as a timeless trailblazer in the history of medical science.
